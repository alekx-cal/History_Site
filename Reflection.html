Agricultural activities of the ancient Maya show that they respected and appreciated nature tremendously by observing the cycles in nature and the skies as opposed to ceremonious activity. Their engineered methods were new ways of doing things like the planting of crops known as the three sisters approach and planting according to seasons due to trends by the moon which reflected sustainable food production which could sustain large numbers of people and suitable to different ecological habitat in Belize. The modern day descendants of the Maya agriculture culture (represented in the places and in the names of the ancient places such as the Caracol and Lamanai) are still having some effect over the cultural aspects of the present day Belize, with the common belief of having a healthy correlation with the land, which is an aspect that the present day society needs to learn. By appreciating the complexity of such practices, we break stereotyping of the Maya and remind about the current significance of traditional ecological knowledge in solving modern problems of environment and land rights in Belize. This sense of connection with ancient knowledge makes us value the concept of sustainable life more deeply and find the motivation to make steps to preserve the natural environment nowadays.
