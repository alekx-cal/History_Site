Welcome to The Third Analysis

The prevailing view, held since the early part of the 20th century and supported by scholars such as Eric Thompson and Sylvanus Morley, was that the Maya were subsistent to a great extent on an agrarian system based on slash-and-burn (swidden or milpa) centered principally on maize, beans, and squash, with a scattered rural populace dwelling near ceremonial centers. This model purported thin spreads of the population and simple agricultural techniques. Later archaeological reconnaissance, particularly in the late 1950s around Tikal, disclosed densely populated suburbs and smaller nearby centers, which defied the premise that only swidden agriculture could support such higher densities. Maya farming in Belize was also very responsive, familiar with the environment, and rooted in cultural tradition. Techniques varied from milpa polycultures to constructed wetland canals and terraces, each adapted to particular ecological niches. Such systems maintained high population densities for centuries and showed high adaptability to environmental change. But ecological pressure was indeed a fact: population growth and climate stress sometimes stretched these systems to their limits. The double pressure of colonial and modern land policies and crew subsistence was no longer the sole game in town causing growing tension. The battle for the recognition of land rights and traditional ecological management continues to be relevant to Belize today.
